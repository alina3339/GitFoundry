The argument, briefly

A shop window
is not the same thing
as a shop.

A Facebook page, if we are honest about what it is, is a shop window on a very busy street. People walk past it — mostly people who already know you — and occasionally one of them stops to look in. It works, in its way. Regulars find you. Word of mouth travels. You post a picture of the new menu, the finished kitchen, the week's offer, and a handful of people tap a heart.

But a shop window is not a shop. And somewhere in the last ten years, something quiet and important changed. The phrase "I'll look them up" stopped meaning search Facebook. It came to mean, almost without exception, Google.

When a stranger in your town needs a plumber at nine o'clock on a Tuesday evening — or a hairdresser before a wedding, or a café that opens early on a Sunday — they do not open Facebook. They open Google. They type what they need, followed by the name of the town, and they expect to see websites. Not profiles. Not posts. Websites.

Google, politely but firmly, does not put Facebook pages first. It puts them somewhere near the bottom, if it puts them there at all. The business without a website is not in the conversation. It is not, in any meaningful sense, visible.

This produces a small and ongoing loss that is nearly impossible to feel — because the customer you lost was never yours to begin with. They searched, found nothing, and booked someone else. They were not rude enough to tell you they looked. You will never meet them. You will simply, month after month, get slightly fewer calls than the business down the road whose name appears, tidily, at the top of the results.

A website is not a grander, fancier version of a Facebook page. It is a different object entirely: a small, permanent building on the street where strangers are actively walking past, looking in — and deciding, in roughly four seconds, whether you look like the sort of business they were hoping to find.

Five things a Facebook page cannot do

The quiet losses
you are probably absorbing.

None of these are dramatic. That is the point. A business rarely goes under because of a missing website; it simply grows, month by month, at a rate slightly lower than it might have.

Search
You don't appear when strangers look for you The person who types "emergency plumber Stoke-on-Trent" or "wedding photographer Staffordshire" is a customer who has already decided to spend money. Without a website, your business is not on the list they're choosing from. Your competitor's is.
Trust
A website is the modern equivalent of a proper shopfront When a potential customer — especially one over forty, or one paying a meaningful amount of money — looks at two businesses, one with a website and one without, they read the difference as established versus not quite sure yet. It is not fair, but it is how most people quietly decide.
Reach
Not everyone is on Facebook — and not everyone who is, sees you A large portion of the British public either doesn't use Facebook or uses it rarely. Of the ones who do, Facebook's algorithm shows your posts to only a fraction of your own followers. Your shop window, it turns out, is behind frosted glass.
Control
You are renting — and the landlord sets the rules Facebook can change what your page looks like, limit its reach, suspend it, or decide it has broken a rule you did not know existed. It has done all of these things to other small businesses, without warning, without appeal. A website you own cannot be taken away from you.
The simple things
Prices, opening hours, directions, a proper contact form The small, practical things a customer looks for before they decide — all of them live awkwardly on a Facebook page, buried under posts. On a website they sit where they belong: visible, on the page, in front of the person who was about to call you.
A small thought experiment

One job.
That's the whole maths.

Consider, for a moment, the kind of job your business tends to take on. A boiler repair. A haircut and colour. A family photography session. A month of window cleaning. A set of bookkeeping for a small firm.

Now consider the value of just one of those jobs — the one job you would take on this year that you otherwise wouldn't have, because a stranger found your website on a Tuesday evening.

For most trades and small businesses, that single job is worth between £150 and several thousand pounds. A professional website starts at £149. It is built once. There is no monthly fee.

The arithmetic, viewed this way, is almost embarrassingly in the website's favour. It is not a question of whether it pays for itself. It is a question of how quickly.
